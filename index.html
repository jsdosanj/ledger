The enterprise layer Obsidian doesn't have

Obsidian is brilliant for a single person. The moment a company depends on those notes, you need governance Obsidian was never built to give. That layer is the product.

Shared org vault + roles

One company vault with real roles & permissions — who can read, edit, approve, or publish each space. Admin, Editor, Reviewer, Viewer, Portal. Obsidian has no concept of who's allowed to touch what.

Review & approval governance

Every page carries an owner and a last-reviewed date. Ledger flags stale docs, records sign-off, and an edit re-opens approval. Documentation auditors will actually trust.

Read-only published portals

Share a space with non-editors and auditors as a self-contained read-only portal — they read, they can't change a thing, and they need no account.

Org-wide search + links graph

Search-as-you-type across every space — titles, tags, frontmatter, body. Plus a links/graph view: backlinks and "what depends on this?", the connection map Obsidian draws, made navigable.

The Obsidian → company-grade flow

  1. Import your vault — paste or point Ledger at a folder of .md. Frontmatter, folders, tags and [[wikilinks]] carry over. (A sample KB is preloaded to explore.)
  2. Browse spaces on the left rail — the "spine" — grouped by folder, with a tag cloud and org-wide search.
  3. Open a page — rendered Markdown, a frontmatter table, wiki-links, backlinks, and a Links view showing what depends on it.
  4. Govern it — see the owner and last-reviewed status; mark reviewed today; a Reviewer approves the version.
  5. Switch roles (Admin / Editor / Reviewer / Viewer / Portal) to see exactly what each can do — edit, approve, publish, or read-only.
  6. Publish a space as a read-only portal — one self-contained HTML file an auditor opens with no account, no editing.
  7. Export any page or the whole vault as portable Markdown — open it back in Obsidian. No lock-in by design.
